# Athléta

*The defining characteristic of an entrepreneur is that we can talk ourselves into anything. Luckily, I talked myself into starting a phenomenal market—big, underserved, and growing fast—before knowing anything about it.*

— Scott W. Kerslake, President and CEO of Athleta

---

On the morning of March 26, 2002, Scott Kerslake—president and CEO of Athleta—reflected on the unfamiliar sounds of the city while stuck in a Manhattan traffic jam. In contrast, several employees at the Athleta headquarters in Petaluma, California, were preparing to run in the open preserve near the office or bike their dogs for a walk. Kerslake and Joe Teno—Athleta's senior vice president of operations—were in New York City with a specific goal: they had been meeting with Series B round of financing from investors who understood and appreciated Athleta's business model and culture. Over the past week, they were determined to secure a commitment for a Series E round of financing of approximately $3.43 million.

In 1999, Kerslake founded Athleta as a "women's sports company" selling a wide range of athletic apparel to female athletes through mail-order catalogs and the Internet. After surviving the dot-com bust, Athleta was almost universally perceived to reach almost $16 million in 2002. Now, Athleta needed Series E funding to expand its business into physical retail outlets. Between 1998 and the end of 2002, Athleta expected to have fulfilled over 400,000 orders, acquired a total of 200,000 customers, and generated over $55 million in revenues—all with under $8 million in equity capital. Despite Athleta's historical and present-day underperformance, Athleta had developed what Kerslake considered to be the ideal platform for becoming a dominant and large company. Before leaving New York, Kerslake needed to develop a sound financing plan.

---

## Founder's Vision

Kerslake had always dreamed about starting his own company. Prior to becoming an entrepreneur, Kerslake had spent time working in management positions, including as director of sales at a retail company before joining Sapient Corporation as a management consultant in 1993. There, Kerslake managed a team of 12, served as principal of a consulting project for Levi Strauss, and was charged with overseeing web-based development at Sapient. In 1997, he moved to Esprit, where he served as Sapient's director of marketing and was instrumental in the company's successful initial public offering. While at Esprit, Kerslake conceived his idea for a women's sporting goods company. He explained:

*I have always loved sports and lived a very active life. Sports have been important to my physical and spiritual well-being for as long as I can remember—so the business model is somewhat autobiographical.*

Then, one day, I had a dream—literally speaking—about building a women's sports company. After thinking about it, I identified a huge gap between what women wanted to do and what you could afford to do. The only way to bridge the gap is through sweat, hard work, and execution. Starting from scratch, I was willing to try it. Esprit was a great company, but I wanted to build a company with integrity. So, we stuck to our guns, retained control of the business, and found a team of people who truly love sports. To keep the business running well, because companies like Esprit/Go BarGroup burned through tens of millions and then closed their doors.

Kerslake then set out to develop Athleta's business plan. His first external round was to angel investors at a premoney valuation of $6.8 million in March 1999. (See **Exhibit 3** for Athleta's financial statements.) By 2002, Athleta's management team, including its head of Quality and Human Resources, Director of New Customer Acquisition, and senior manager roles for merchandise and accessories. The management team operated a staff of 35 employees.

In 1999, a total of 21.2 million women and 15.5 million men in the U.S. participated in sports and fitness. Women's participation in sports and fitness had grown rapidly in the past decade: by 1999, women made up 43% of the 47.1 million Americans who participated in outdoor sporting activities, a figure that was expected to reach 50% by 2000. There were 15.4 million paid health club memberships in the United States in 2001.

## Competition

There were four categories of retailers that sold women's athletic gear (see Exhibit 2 for a detailed list of Athleta's competitors by category):

- Tier 1 : Women-specific sports retailers concentrating exclusively on the women's market (e.g., Lady Foot Locker, Title 9)
- Tier 2 : Specialty sporting goods retailers concentrating on a particular sport (e.g., running or cycling sports) but also carrying related products and offering customers a higher-level of expertise (e.g., Foot Feet, Recreation Equipment Incorporated)
- Tier 3 : Big box retailers carrying very broad and deep product selections for many different sports (e.g., Sports Authority, Gart Sports)
- Tier 4 : Department stores carrying a narrow range of sports-related products (e.g., Nordstrom, Macy's)

## Channel Strategy

Athleta was launched as a multichannel company serving customers through the mail-order and catalog channels. The company planned to expand its stores in 2003 after proving its expertise in catalog and online retailing. By the end of 2001, Athleta had generated over 80% of revenue from its mail catalog and 16% of revenues from the Athleta.com Web site. While many experts were concerned that e-commerce cannibalized other channels, Athleta's management team believed that this catalog/Internet combination actually fostered stronger customer relationships and it led to a larger catalog presence near the office or bike their dogs for a walk.

Kerslake conceptualized Athleta as a premier women's multichannel sports retailer offering a limited selection of top products across a wide variety of sports and an exceptional level of customer service.

## Product

The initial product line was sourced from Athleta Essentials—a private-label line of bodywear (a technical term for athletic underwear). The Athleta brand would eventually encompass the product lines of over 200 brands. In 2002, Athleta carried 150 distinct brands, 75% of which were private label. We designed our own line of clothing because our customers were requesting items, colors, and styles that weren't being made. One of the things we heard most often was that it's really tough to find high-quality sports bras, so we set out to meet that need. We focused on developing and maintaining strategic supplier relationships; in particular, Athleta concentrated on manufacturing-and-selling Athleta private-label sportswear and adding additional customer service benefits.

There are two models for private-label branding: either sell a knockoff of an existing product at a lower price or develop your own distinctive product. Many retailers will take top-selling vendor products and put private labels on them. We took the other approach: we created unique private-label products that were better and more expensive than the private-label alternatives. They were on par with other high-quality manufacturers. While we could have chased a lot of sales by reverse-engineering low-end carryback, we didn't want to compromise the Athleta brand.

## Customer

Athleta's initial target customers were women between the ages of 18 and 50 with a college education and average household incomes greater than $80,000. They were also physically active and participated in two or more sporting activities. Most of Athleta's customers were professionals and career women, as well as wives and mothers. Kerslake said that Athleta physically sought demographics for their names. Initially, we purchased lists from a database company. We were paying on average $0.10 per name for a list, which represents 75,000–100,000 names per list from a single mail campaign. We did not include the names of 30-year-old women. We were managed to send catalogs to the right people. By the end of 1999, our house file included over 3,000 customer names which grew to 7,400 by 2000 and 9,700 by 2001.

The Athleta brand was designed to communicate an understanding of and respect for the importance of sports and athletics in women's lives. As such, the company's catalogs featured fit, attractive models actively engaged in sports settings, which Kerslake said provided customers with a feeling of camaraderie, community, and belonging. Kerslake also noted that Athleta catalogs tried to provide accurate information, give thoughtful advice, and understand the particular needs of women in specific sporting activities. The result was high customer loyalty: Kerslake reported that 46% of Athleta's revenue in 2001 came from repeat customers.

## Culture

Athleta worked hard to create an exceptional culture where the employees embraced the company's first core values. The company values all stemmed from a single premise: the most important thing you can do is create a world where athletic women feel understood and celebrated. For example, Athleta employees were encouraged to put their families first even though this occasionally lowered productivity levels. Furthermore, Athleta employees had an informal relationship with the open preserve so that employees could easily go morning or cycling during breaks. The employee-run newsletter was named after the office dog. The management team also recognized sports contributions in key positions. Finally, the open-concept Athleta office was designed to facilitate open communication and to accommodate employees, pets, and, on occasion, children.

## Company History

After developing the Athleta concept and performing market research on the women's sports apparel market, Kerslake set out to raise his first external round of financing for the company. In 1998, he raised a total of $769,000 and invested the money in producing the company's first catalog, completing Athleta's first Web site, and hiring its first full-time employees. Kerslake was then in the process of acquiring Athleta's first customers:

*Before we had a Web site, I tried to exchange names with a database company if you have a house file with your own customer names, you can pay a large lot compile such as $10 for 100,000 names. We tried to find companies with similar demographics for their names. Initially, we purchased lists from a database company. We were paying on average $0.10 per name, which represents 75,000–100,000 names from a single mail campaign.*

However, at the end of 1998, nearly all of the seed capital Kerslake had raised was exhausted. He needed Series A capital to continue building the business. The market climate was excellent in 1998: Kerslake decided to raise additional Series A capital from a small number of venture capital firms. Kerslake recalled:

*From the beginning, I realized that there is a large gap between what you aspire to do and what you can afford to do. The only way to bridge the gap is through sweat, hard work, and execution. Starting from scratch, I was willing to try it. Esprit was a great company, but I wanted to build a company with integrity. So, we stuck to our guns, retained control of the business, and found a team of people who truly love sports. To keep the business running well, because companies like Esprit/Go BarGroup burned through tens of millions and then closed their doors.*

### 1. Increase circulation to acquire a larger base of customers

The catalog business depended heavily upon customer response rates to mailings. In general, it cost Athleta approximately $0.60 to print and mail each catalog. Athleta sought to achieve a 3.5% response rate on prospects<sup>3</sup> and an average order size of $115.00 in order to break even (given 2002 cost structure and gross margin levels). For fiscal year 2002, Athleta's management team estimated revenues of $16 million. Management believed there were at least 20 million customers in Athleta's target market.

### 2. Improve supplier terms

Beginning in 2000, Athleta paid its suppliers on an extended basis, forgoing early-payment discounts as large as 8% on cost of goods sold (COGS). While extending supplier payments enabled Athleta to preserve cash (resulting in a balance of approximately $2.3 million), it reduced EBIT margins considerably. If Athleta could pay suppliers in advance, it could improve margins by approximately $11.00 for each back order and an annual inventory turnover of four compared with the industry average of 2.2 times per year. Prepaying suppliers would save Athleta money on payment terms with suppliers and reduce inventory management costs.

### 3. Upgrade management information systems (MIS)

Due to Athleta's rapid growth, it had outgrown its current headquarters and 9,000-square-foot distribution center. Athleta also needed to upgrade its MIS in order to provide more detailed database tracking to identify and target customers. Athleta required $200,000 for moving expenses and $380,000 for distribution infrastructure.

### 4. Expand Athleta's private-label line

Since its inception, Athleta's private-label brand filled a niche in the women's sportswear market at price-to-value that only Athleta could offer. By 2002, the private label represented 20% of total product assortment in 2002. In addition, Athleta's private-label line frequently outsold the best products from well-known brands such as Nike and The North Face, and it was produced by high-quality manufacturers. Athleta projected that expanding its private-label sales would increase EBIT margins by 40–45% of total assortment and 45% of total sales revenue.

### 5. Team expansion and marketing initiatives

Finally, Athleta intended to hire new staff and invest in new marketing initiatives. To begin, Athleta needed to hire three people to expand its catalog and online capabilities and one person to invest in the customer service, distribution, and Web development departments. Kerslake added that he needed to hire one person specifically to deal with retailers. Finally, Athleta's total marketing budget would increase to $700,000 in 2003. This budget would include: (a) agency partnerships and co-marketing agreements, and (b) co-op advertising. Kerslake estimated that $220,000 was required to hire additional employees and to invest in the technology infrastructure.

## Financing Options

When Kerslake and Teno went to New York in late March 2002, Athleta had less than $50,000 in cash remaining. However, several different options emerged during their fund-raising trip to the East Coast.

**Venture capital** During the first quarter of 2002, the VC market continued a cyclical downturn that had started in early 2001 (see **Exhibit 5** for U.S. venture capital investment trends). In particular, the consumer category was especially hard hit—with over 1,400 consumer-oriented companies and hundreds of companies declaring bankruptcy because they could not develop a sustainable economic model. In general, Kerslake noted, early-stage VCs had developed a deep understanding of investments in consumer retail businesses and applied even higher discount rates to revenue projections. Most consumer retail VCs had exited the market. Some of the most active VCs with whom Kerslake had spoken remained active in the consumer retail sector. However, the VCs he met were interested primarily in companies with clear exit opportunities; they wanted to work with companies to go public or be acquired by most fund. To make matters worse, consumer retail companies had fallen out of favor in the market. Exit options for consumer retail companies had been substantially diminished. Therefore, exit options for retailers in the consumer sector were limited to being acquired or merging with another retail company.

Kerslake also noted one characteristic that distinguished VC from other investment sources:

*It is really difficult to find a VC firm interested in specialty retail companies—especially with the economics of retail. The VCs we met appreciated our model and found it compelling. What struck us that they invested in proven concepts, strong management teams, and aggressive growth. They had also shifted to companies in other industries due to the dot-com bust. The venture capitalists were still making investments but with rigorous terms (i.e., more stringent anti-dilution provisions). Their deal had decreased substantially, so they were willing to move quickly when they found the right opportunity.*

**Angel investors** By 2002, there were literally thousands of active angel investors in the U.S. These groups were typically former CEOs and senior managers with experience in running growth-stage private companies. While in New York, Kerslake and Teno met with a handful of angel investors who were interested in Athleta. Some angels were high-profile consumer retail executives; others approached other potential angel investors including professional athletes, female celebrities, and former competitive cyclists. In general, most angels were interested in investing $750,000–$2 million in Athleta.

Because of our cash crunch, I was concerned about the time and effort required to raise equity from a group of angels. Given that most angel investors invest between $50,000 and $1 million, Kerslake believed that the right type of angel investors would be those who could bring something beyond pure money to Athleta's business model—they wanted to be "partners" rather than just "investors" in the company.

Another financing option available to negotiate credit lines with one or several **commercial banks**—specifically with their growth companies. The requirement to raise $1.5 million to $2.5 million from $750,000 to $2 million in credit facilities. However, the credit markets had tightened during 2002, and it was difficult for growth-stage companies to raise debt. As Kerslake explained:

*Raising a small amount of debt would buy us enough time to raise the Series E from other sources. Unfortunately, commercial banks were very tight-fisted in 2002.*

Kerslake was eager to develop a sound financing plan for Athleta. Every day, his company helped hundreds of women find athletic apparel that met their needs. And, Kerslake was convinced that bringing Athleta into physical retail outlets would open up dozens of new opportunities for the company to serve female athletes. Now all he had to do was secure the capital necessary to take Athleta to the next level.
