Rob Bernshteyn was looking forward to the weekend and some free time to think about the year ahead for Coupa, the software as a service (SaaS) firm he ran in San Mateo, California. Coupa was a young, VC-backed venture that had developed a successful platform in the “eProcurement” space. Coupa offered a service that helped companies consolidate and manage their purchasing of “indirect” goods and supplies, i.e., items that were not part of a company’s costs of goods sold but were used in general and administrative activities. For example, instead of having 12 different office managers in 12 different office locations negotiate and buy copier paper or bottled water from different suppliers, Coupa’s platform directed those purchases to a common pre-selected vendor, electronically routed the purchase requests to the appropriate manager(s), generated a purchase order and, ultimately, a payment request and approval.

Bernshteyn had become Coupa’s CEO after some of its early struggles, and in four years had grown the company from a few hundred thousand dollars in revenue to a $12 million (recurring revenue) business. He had helped the company raise $41.5 million in VC. The company was a leading competitor in this growing space. Now —in January, 2013—there were important issues on the table around the company’s growth strategy:

We can continue our organic growth, put some more feet on the street and ramp up sales as we have been doing. Alternatively, we could be much more aggressive, doubling down on this organic strategy and hiring more salespeople and even expanding overseas. There is also an opportunity to acquire some of the small players in this space at an attractive price, and immediately bring that revenue to our P&L. And of course, there is always the option of exiting as other, bigger players see this opportunity and view us as an attractive foothold.

Background: SaaS and the eProcurement Space

With the advent of the Internet and ubiquitous broadband access, it became possible for users to access software that was not resident on their computer, but which resided in “the cloud.” It took some time for business users to get comfortable having their data on a third-party computer network, but the advantages made for a compelling argument. There were far fewer issues around installation, version control, upgrades or service than existed in the classic desktop software environment. A single, up-to date version of the software simply lived on the servers of the software provider, and was accessed by the user. This came to be known as “software as a service” (SaaS) and was typically purchased on a monthly or annual subscription basis by the user. Early competitors in the

---

eProcurement space included Ariba, which built an early client-server based product. Bernshteyn offered a perspective on the early activity in the space:

Ariba’s first product was focused on the same indirect spend arena that Coupa ultimately pursued. But then, with the success of eBay, people saw big potential in the business to business (B-to-B) supply chain management space. With eBay's rapid growth proving there was a fast-growing network effect of helping buyers find sellers, the logic went that the same dynamics in the larger B-to-B market would command a similar valuation multiple. Ariba climbed to a $40 billion market cap at the height of the dot com boom. And when it went bust, Ariba dropped to a $1 billion market cap. So, that pretty well poisoned the space for a while - no one would invest in eProcurement.

Salesforce.com was one of the early successes in the SaaS space serving companies with a streamlined set of sales force management tools via the cloud. Other notable successes in the space included NetSuite, Concur and Cornerstone OnDemand.

Coupa’s value proposition had several elements:

© Gather a higher fraction of indirect spending under common control/ oversight so it could be more effectively managed;

* Lower average costs of goods ordered by pooling purchases previously made across multiple vendors; and,

“We do well in the upper middle market— medium-sized to large companies. The really small competitors can’t scale up to serve these companies, and the bigger competitors can’t scale down. We also do well with “distributed” users—the software is easy to deploy and use, so it’s well suited to being used by a wide swath of employees far from headquarters.”

Coupa had well over 250 customers that ranged from small, single-location businesses like Pandora to large retail chains with thousands of locations. McDonalds, for instance had 3,000 locations on the system and the large insurer Chubb, 5,000.

Bernshteyn explained how Coupa priced its products:

Pricing is based on the number of people in the organization covered by the buying activity. For example, a company of 500 people might have 10 admins who did all the indirect purchasing, and we'd get roughly $75,000 per year for serving that account. We typically sign companies up for 2-3 year contracts. Typically there is a one-time set-up fee averaging 30%-40% of the first year Annual Contract Value (or “ACV,” which was the annual recurring revenue from a customer contract, excluding the one-time set-up fee). Average deal size is about $150,000 per year, with the smallest customers at about $20,000 and the largest a bit over $1,000,000.

One of the keys to the Coupa offering was that it gave management visibility on what was happening: “We went to a big investment bank, and they saved $100,000 the first week just on their purchases of bottled water. Salesforce.com saved $10 million in the first year rejecting purchases of ineligible goods and services that would otherwise have been made. Other companies are able to cut out administrative headcount because the process becomes so streamlined.”

Sales and Marketing

Coupa had two different selling organizations: the “enterprise” sales team sold to businesses with more than 2,000 to 2,500 people, and the “mid-market” (MM) team sold to businesses of smaller size. Bernshteyn described the sales process:

On the enterprise side, there is a sales cycle of 4 to 5 months. The average enterprise deal is for 3 years at $500,000 per year, with a $150,000 to $200,000 one-time fee. The average quota on the enterprise side is between $800,000 and $1,000,000 per year. These folks get their leads via trade shows, customer references, our own lead gen process, and old-fashioned cold-calling. On the mid-market side, the deals average a bit less than $100,000 and the quotas are correspondingly lower. Often, we can close a deal like this over the phone. As of the end of 2012, we have 10 enterprise sales reps and 7 middle market, and a group of another dozen or so people who support them.

Most of our salespeople come from a background in sales with specializations in finance, procurement, or expense management. Note that we pay commission on “annual contract value.” Let's say a $600,000 contract is signed on December 1. We pay the salesperson a commission based on the $600,000 ACV. But, under GAAP, we can only recognize one-twelfth — $50,000 —in that calendar year, and the rest in the following year. On average, we are paying commission on 100 cents on the dollar of ACV, but recognizing significantly less in the year in which the contract is signed. That is because sales are skewed towards the last half of the year—in part because that is when customers tend to spend, and in part, because we are growing rapidly, so sales are naturally higher later in the year. On the plus side, we pay commission only one time on that sale. We don’t pay any commission on renewals, but there is always the incentive of increasing ACV at existing customers, which we believe keeps the salesperson involved in their accounts. Our average renewal rate is 95%, and with a 5% or so price increase each year, we keep 100% of our revenue/contract value in successive years, and are not paying any commission on those follow-on years.

The Coupa Sales Model

Exhibit 3 describes the Coupa sales and revenue model. The model takes as inputs the number of sales reps that are hired over time, and shows how they move through the system to become productive sellers, and what they book each quarter, and how those ACV bookings translate into GAAP revenue. It relies on a number of assumptions some of which are slightly simplified for the sake of the model:

New reps take 6 months to “ramp” and become productive. About 65% succeed in “ramping” and about 35% wash out and leave before they ever become productive. Of ramped reps, about 3% leave each quarter for other opportunities. Thus, the new reps hired each quarter take 2 quarters before they really have an impact on sales revenue.

Ramped reps cost about $90,000 per quarter, unramped reps about $35,000 per quarter, and the cost of a new hire is about $55,000 for recruiters’ fees and other one-time expenses. In addition to these expenses that varied according to the number of reps, there were other fixed expenses of the sales function.

Once they became productive, ramped reps were targeted to hit about $625,000 of ACV in 2013, and about $645,000 in ACV in 2014. That ACV was spread unevenly over the 4

quarters in a year, based on the budgeting and implementation cycles of Coupa’s customers, with far more sales in the second and especially the fourth quarter.

* The impact of $1 of newly acquired ACV on quarterly GAAP revenue (for the quarter in which it was signed) was about $0.083 (one-twelfth). GAAP required booking the revenue in the month in which the service was actually delivered, so—on an annual contract—12 months’ worth of ACV was spread evenly over the succeeding 12 months. Because the quarter was, of course, shorter than a year, the company couldn't book a full year’s worth of revenue in any quarter anyway. But sales tended to happen disproportionately towards the end of the quarter, so the fraction of booked ACV that turned into quarterly GAAP revenue was even less. For example, if Coupa signed a contract worth $120,000 of ACV on March 1, then it could book one-twelfth—$10,000—as Q1 revenue. However, fully one- quarter of the ACV—$30,000—rolled forward to become part of Q2’s new recurring revenue base. In general, the company started each quarter with a revenue base that then grew by 8.3% (one-twelfth) of the ACV booked in that quarter. On a cash basis, however, Coupa usually collected the full year’s value of the contract within 30 days of signing.

Bernshteyn explained: “As you can tell, it is a complex equation to manage. You want to grow as quickly as you can, subject to the organization's ability to execute and to managing the economics — You don’t want to blow the business model economics. A high growth rate helps the valuation, but not if your underlying metrics are collapsing.”

Coupa: Planning for 2013 — How and How Fast To Grow

In the fall of 2012, Bernshteyn and his team began working on their planning and budgeting for the following year. The process brought certain strategic questions to a head —namely, what growth target to set and what kind of strategy to use to achieve it.

The team developed a baseline plan (see Exhibit 3 for the 2013 and 2014 plan and the key assumptions behind it) calling for 2013 sales revenue of slightly over $21 million, and modeling 2014 revenues of roughly $35 million. This put Coupa on a growth path (for recurring revenue) in the 65% range, a target that gave Bernshteyn optimism about the kind of multiple Coupa could garner in an IPO: “The investment bankers are already in here making their pitch and talking about growth rates and valuations in the sector. A high growth rate for recurring revenue gives us a lot of control over our own destiny, whether it is an IPO, a strategic M&A transaction, or simply raising more capital at a strong valuation.” High growth recurring revenue was one of the keys to the SaaS model. While the costs of customer acquisition might be high, the cost to keep customers on the platform once deployed were very measured. Therefore, the fundamentals underlying a valuable company were highly correlated with the growth rate of recurring revenue. The fact is, it recurs without much additional effort. (See Exhibit 5 for a list of some SAAS firms and their financial and operating statistics and valuation metrics.)

This baseline case was the “Organic Growth” option, which was based upon growing the sales force. Coupa had added 2 or 3 reps per quarter during 2012, growing the sales force to about 18 by year end 2012. The company’s plan for 2013 was to hire about 20 reps, growing the sales force to more than 32 by year end. “Growing the sales force each year is the classic growth model for young companies at our stage.” A more aggressive version of the organic growth strategy would be to simply add more sales people: “We could grow our sales force from 20 to 60 or so. That would include expanding internationally to a couple of key cities—Sydney, Frankfurt, Amsterdam perhaps. We'd have to be careful —we don’t want to hire a bunch of unproductive people. But I think we could pull this off.”

---

Roll-up strategy: Another strategy—one that could be carried out in conjunction with organic growth—was to acquire smaller companies in this space:

There are 15 or 20 small companies out there that we could buy pretty easily. They have $2 to $5 million of top-line revenue. A few of them even have features and functionality that we could integrate with our product and grow our suite. These are companies that started earlier than us, but grew more slowly and didn’t take VC. We should be able to buy these companies at 2 or 3 times revenues, especially if we add a small equity kicker. Generally, we could integrate their revenues into our P&L at 50% plus gross margins, and keep a good fraction of their sales and marketing team productive. Most of the admin and tech spend would go away. This would obviously deplete our cash. We could probably even get some debt to do these deals.

Bernshteyn also believed that there were other good reasons to consolidate the space: “The customer is really waiting for a leader to emerge. They want to rally around a winner. It makes it easier to justify their purchase decision to their boss, and makes them more confident that the SaaS provider will be around in the future to service, support and enhance the product.” The downside of course is that the product gets integrated together vs. being built organically from the ground up, which had technical and usability value.

As 2012 ended, Coupa was burning about $1 million per month, and Bernshteyn believed that “each dollar of new first year revenue costs us about $1 of sales and marketing spend.”

Exit: Of course, another option that was always on the table—at least conceptually —was an exit. Bernshteyn talked about the ultimate game plan:

Of course, an IPO is the highest value outcome for investors. We are shooting for $21 million of GAAP revenue next year under our baseline scenario, and that would take us to over $35 million the year after. I think we could go file to go public if we hit our targets for the following year.

Alternatively, there are—naturally—big companies out there who see this as an attractive space: Oracle, IBM, Concur, even Salesforce.com. There would be a lot of synergy —and thus value—in rolling out our product to their existing clients, along with adding our product to the list of modules their sales forces offer new customers.
