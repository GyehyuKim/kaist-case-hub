DexAI

“I have good news and bad news,” attorney Meredith Grey said on an April 2023 afternoon. “You have two term sheets from two great firms, but they reflect today’s tough financing market.”

DexAI co-founders Elizabeth Evermore (“Betty”) and James L. Pond were driving in Evermore’s car and speaking with their attorney through speakerphone. They felt both exhausted and excited: after a lengthy fundraising process that involved contacting over 500 potential investors, two venture-capital ("VC") investors had offered term sheets. But they became concerned as Grey continued: “I just emailed you a summary. The economic terms are nearly identical, but there are some legal terms to note. Unfortunately, VCs have the leverage in this down market. After you get back to your office, print out the information and call me.”

They ended the call, and the self-proclaimed “friends for life” glanced at each other. Pond immediately checked his phone and started reading out loud Grey’s summary. After a few minutes, he stopped. “I don’t understand any of this,” Pond said. Evermore said nothing. The introvert of the founding team, she was known to her friends and family for two things: wearing cardigan sweaters and her infrequent and very thoughtful comments. Pond was used to this silence and let it continue.

Finally, Evermore spoke. “We have one of the best AI technologies out there, and we have one of the best start-up lawyers. What we cannot do is turn down both term sheets. We must get the money and get going. And we're both tired of fundraising.”

‘As the car slowly traveled amidst the rush-hour traffic on Route 101 northbound to San Francisco, Pond started to muse out loud about what they should get for dinner. Evermore was lost in thought and began to ruminate. “What do we decide, and how do we start-up rookies do it?” she thought.

“Friends for Life”

Evermore and Pond met at Pennsylvania's West Reading Elementary Center when both were in the first grade and seven years old. They became close friends while attending Wyomissing Area Junior/Senior High School and taking the same Advanced Placement classes for students who scored high on standardized tests. In high school, they began a romantic relationship that lasted three years until it ended abruptly during the summer after their graduation, at which point they ceased all contact with one another.

---

Evermore went to a prestigious California university in the Bay Area on a full scholarship to study Electrical Engineering and Applied Mathematics. Pond matriculated at a research-oriented state university in Illinois and majored in Computer Science, a discipline for which his school was well-known.

The two reconnected at their five-year high school reunion in 2018. Both were surprised that they lived only a few miles from each other. Evermore was working on a PhD in Electrical Engineering at her Bay Area university. Her studies focused on the architectural advances of graphics processing units (“GPUs”). She worked on processors that had multiple “cores” and could conduct a very large number of simultaneous calculations. GPUs were used for several increasingly popular uses, such as video games, video editing and animation, cryptocurrency mining, and virtual reality.

Pond was working at a Big Tech research lab and focused on natural language processing techniques. He was particularly interested in computation models that could learn and make decisions over time via machine learning algorithms. This area had great potential because computer neural networks advanced materially as computer memory and processing costs declined.

Evermore and Pond began to meet informally over coffee and meals over the ensuing years as each continued to work on their fields. “Betty and I again became very close,” Pond said. “We talk about everything except each other's dating lives. We are life-long friends, I have no doubt.” “We are never, ever getting back together, but we are like two peas from the same pod,” Evermore said. “Because of our common past, we speak in short code. It's very efficient.”

Forming the Idea: They Had a Marvelous Time

Over midnight pizza one night during the winter holidays of 2020, Pond suggested that the two start a company together. Venture capital activity was quite robust, and discussions about web3 and cryptocurrency seemed to dominate every work and personal gathering that Pond attended. Evermore was eager to try something new, as her PhD work while alone and isolated during the COVID-19 lockdown made her realize that she needed a change. She suggested that they think about a new Artificial Intelligence (“AI”) company. With her knowledge of hardware and his software insights, she felt they together had a unique and differentiated point of view.

A few months later, Evermore took a leave of absence from her PhD program and Pond resigned from his job. They secured cheap space at a co-working facility, decided on “DexAI” as a name, and white-boarded some ideas. The founders’ hypothesis about “why DexAI and why them” centered on Evermore's knowledge of cutting-edge hardware that would enable the new company to ingest, store, and parse very large amounts of data very quickly and Pond’s expertise in self-learning software and large-language models that would automate DexAI.

They increasingly focused on one idea, winnowed down from an initial 13: could they leverage proprietary AI hardware and software to create a new enterprise revenue platform? By analyzing a company’s social media, sales, marketing, and customer service data, DexAI theoretically could identify data patterns much faster than humans did. What if it automatically identified gross profit opportunities and implemented action steps to capture them? The latter was possible. Many software programs already offered Application Program Interfaces ("APIs") that permitted interactions, but what was missing was a highly intelligent software agent on the other side to make decisions and act upon.

---

‘As the founders continued their research, Evermore concluded that DexAI could automate certain labor-intensive business processes; for example, it could identify a list of customer prospects, score them to see which were most likely to buy, contact the best prospects via email, send a short survey to qualify a customer's interest, and then schedule a Zoom call with a sales representative. Moreover, Pond thought such a platform could self-learn over time and continuously get better on its own.

In addition, Evermore wanted DexAI to have a novel pricing scheme: charge a small set-up fee but attain most of its revenues through commissions. What if DexAI earned a percentage of the value that it created for customers? Evermore thought this would align incentives and render less-automated competitors obsolete. She thought DexAI could create a new category: Revenue as a Service or “RaaS.”

Over the next few months, the Founders wrote multiple versions of a business plan, a PowerPoint presentation, a technical white paper, and a Product Requirements Document (“PRD”) for DexAI 1.0. The duo conducted field research, interviewed potential customers, funded cheap tests to codify the customer value proposition, and iterated on their documents as they learned new insights.

With the help of Grey, a known and respected Menlo Park attorney who had her choice of clients, they incorporated DexAI in March of 2021. At the same time, Grey structured tax-efficient equity grants for the founders; they each received 50,000 shares of common stock at a value of $0.001 a share.* For vesting, she recommended a company buy back right and that started at 100% and lapsed to 0% in monthly increments over 48 months. For example, if a founder was no longer with the company after 12 months, DexAI would buy back 75% of that founder's total equity position.» Those shares could then be retired or re-sold. To discourage founders from leaving, the buy back price was “at cost,” or $0.001 a share. The founders also agreed to another feature: the buy back right fully lapsed to 0% if DexAI were acquired, merged, or went public. Grey referred to that as “a trigger for 100% acceleration of vesting,”

Fundraising: Running the Gauntlet

The DexAI team started meeting with VCs in November of 2021 and found a great deal of interest. But momentum stalled a few weeks later when the US. stock market began declining. Undaunted, the pair continued to call on investors through the end of 2021. With confidence still high, the founders decided to self-fund DexAI, ultimately investing $300,000 of personal savings into building an extremely preliminary and “feature light” version of their platform. With such little capital, they could

not build out Evermore's advanced hardware architecture, but they did make material progress on the software side. The founders did much of the coding themselves through lengthy hackathons,

The stock market and tech doldrums continued in 2022. From its peak in November of 2021 until December 2022, the NASDAQ declined 35%. One source calculated that 93,000 tech workers were laid off in 2022? One bright spot was that DexAI found ample technical talent willing to work on a project basis and for no equity. Throughout all this, the founders used their own platform to manage a list of over 500 prospective investors, sending out automated content to comment on VCs’ social media posts to try and elicit interest. Each outbound email and social media comment ended with “Automatically powered by DexAI”

ChatGPT launched on November 30, 2022. Soon thereafter VCs replied to DexAI's outbound emails and asked to “hear the pitch and see if I can be helpful.” Evermore said: “The Bay Area went from a web3-crypto party in 2021 to a deep freeze in 2022 to an AI hype-fest by January 2023. We weren’t ready for it”

As momentum increased, the founders decided to forego a $5 million seed round and instead ask for a $15 million Series A. Grey strongly backed that strategy. By early March 2023, Evermore and Pond had pitched 52 VCs and been invited to final round presentations at nine firms. Seven passed, citing that AI was interesting but seemed already crowded very quickly. Fortunately for DexAI, two very well-known VC firms offered term sheets: Cypress Venture Capital and Feline Capital.

Firms and Terms

Cypress had raised over 30 funds in its 50-year history. Cypress XXX, its latest vehicle, was $400 million and reflected the firm’s philosophy of investing “right-sized” funds that targeted high multiples. “They have the best returns in the industry,” an anonymous investor said in a recent newspaper article. Cypress was well-known for replacing founders with a new CEO if growth abated at any of their portfolio companies. Their informal motto: “go big or go home.”

Cypress Managing Partner Javier Rodriguez met with DexAI, pushed to a partners’ decision ‘meeting in a week, and had Cypress portfolio company CEOs text Evermore and Pond to extoll his value-add. Rodriguez had been in VC for over 30 years, a feat for a place as internally competitive as Cypress. A first-generation American, he was well-known for saying this: “no one works harder than someone without a safety net.” Always blunt and candid in all situations, he could be casually cruel in the name of being honest.

Originally a hedge fund, Feline had successfully created a VC business starting in 2010. Its third VC fund was $5 billion. The firm’s investment approach was unique in that it invested in both public and private companies. In VC, the firm covered all stages, from seed to late-stage growth to initial public offerings.

Partner Jane Corrigan met with DexAI and instantly “ got it,” given the firm's own use of AI. Feline employed many developers to “screen scrape” data from LinkedIn, Twitter, and other websites to find growth signals. Which companies posted a sudden jump in open job positions? Which firms had large increases in social media followers? The challenge: the technology went only so far, and there was a great deal of human intervention required to convert the data to actionable information. Brilliant and high-energy, Corrigan maintained a vigorous online presence with her Substack and social media posts. She too asked her CEOs to contact DexAI.

---

On a late Friday afternoon in March of 2023, both Corrigan and Rodriguez independently texted the DexAI founders to expect term sheets within a few hours. Earlier that day, Grey had casually and separately mentioned to each that she thought highly of DexAI and that another well-known VC was interested. Evermore and Pond were ecstatic when the term sheets arrived as promised and immediately forwarded them to Grey, who quickly reviewed and summarized them. She called the DexAI founders, who were stuck together in a traffic jam. They agreed to speak again after the founders returned to their office.

Friday Night Frights

It was 8 pm by the time Evermore and Pond drove through rush hour traffic to their office. They printed out Grey’s materials, read them, and then called their attorney, who generously agreed to skip a dinner party. Grey started the call by saying that she and her partners had done a great deal of work for Cypress and Feline and their companies. “You won't find better VC firms,” she said. “But it’s clear what their concerns are based on the term sheets.”

Over two hours, they talked through the summary documents. She explained to the founders that the VCs wanted to make sure that DexAI cleanly owned its intellectual property (Exhibit 1). They reviewed the other terms by category: economic (Exhibit 2), control (Exhibit 3), and incentives regarding Founder's stock (Exhibit 4a) and employee stock options (Exhibit 4b). They also reviewed the capitalization tables (Exhibit 5).

One surprising topic emerged during the discussion: there was a possibility that Pond had violated an important agreement with his former employer. When hired, Pond signed a customary Invention Disclosure and Assignment Agreement with his employer (Exhibit 6). After some questioning by Grey, Pond realized that he “may” have used his company laptop for a week before he had resigned to do research on DexAI and correspond with Evermore about it. Thereafter, he used a personal laptop.

Grey shared other items in the term sheets: once the founders signed a term sheet, they committed to a 30-day “no shop” period during which the founders could not “solicit, initiate, encourage, or assist” any other VC from submitting a competing offer; the other terms were “non-binding,” meaning that the VCs were not committed to funding the company on those terms until final documents were negotiated and signed; and closing the transaction was contingent on the VCs completing their due diligence. Grey emphasized that these three terms were standard in VC.

Evermore and Pond expressed concern. Sensing this, Grey said:

It's 10 pm, and you both sound tired. Let’s chat again tomorrow because the VCs will want a response soon. The economic terms are ‘market’ and basically identical. But we need to game plan how to negotiate the legal terms. On James’ disclosure agreement, let me get my intellectual property partner involved. We need to figure out a solution and not blow up the term sheets.

Evermore and Pond were silent. Evermore began ruminating on different versions of the same few questions: “Should we disclose the possible intellectual property violation? If so, when should we? Which legal terms should we accept, attempt to modify, or reject?”

It was going to be another long night at DexAI. It was a reality that the two knew all too well. They had even forgotten to order dinner.
