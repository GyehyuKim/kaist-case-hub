## TimeCredit

With her May 2023 Harvard Business School (HBS) graduation looming, Ndonga Sagnia was less than halfway to meeting the fundraising goal for her artificial intelligence (AI) accounting startup. She sat down to meet with her cofounder, Kevin Shabahang, to decide whether to continue dedicating nearly all of her time to raising money or to shift her focus toward developing the company.

Her venture, TimeCredit, aimed to use new AI Large Language Models (LLMs) to streamline the arduous process of creating accounting memos, addressing a pain point Sagnia knew firsthand from her years at Ernst & Young (EY). Unfortunately, she had picked a difficult time to raise capital. Soaring inflation, high interest rates, the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank, and a widespread tech recession had led to a pullback in venture capital (VC) activity and non-committal investors.¹ There was palpable excitement over applications of new AI technology which helped attract investor interest, but most had not yet landed on an investment strategy in the space.² Meanwhile, competition was growing by the day.

Sagnia had secured an early win by landing a reputable launch partner for TimeCredit's prototype as a first customer and source of data to build out their product, but the longer fundraising delayed development of a minimum viable product (MVP), the higher the risk they would lose this early traction. Both their launch partner and Sagnia's technical cofounder, Shabahang, were growing impatient with the lack of progress. Sagnia felt pressure to end the round of fundraising less than halfway to their initial goal of raising $1.5 million, but this also risked leaving them short of the computing and engineering resources they needed to hit their next milestones without running out of money. With Sagnia's graduation looming, Sagnia and Shabahang's budding cofounder relationship faced a difficult early test.

## Building TimeCredit

### The Technical Challenge

Using LLMs to generate accounting memos posed three unique challenges. First and foremost was training and fine-tuning AI models with domain-specific data. Although LLMs performed well for general text generation, accounting required precision and domain-specific expertise. Developing a model that could generate accurate accounting memos required feeding the model accurate, real accounting data. It required the development of LLMs that had both cybersecurity and data privacy protocols to handle sensitive financial information. Without adequate protections, potential enterprise customers — whose financial information was the most sensitive — would not trust the product. For example, in the event of a cybersecurity breach,³ users unlikely to be willing to share their data for training purposes even with explicit protections. Second, the accounting knowledge base needed to be continually updated as accounting practices evolved. Third, scaling the technology to handle large enterprise clients would require significant computing power.

The team focused on the domain expertise that initially motivated Sagnia's business concept. They decided to limit their scope to the accounting domain rather than expanding to other domains like legal, applications, accounting terminology is rich with domain-specific phrases that LLMs needed to correctly contextualize to generate accurate memos. "Accounting knowledge is rich with domain-specific phrases that needed to be correctly contextualized. The most difficulty I had with this was the accounting terminology," Sagnia explained. "I had a lot of people say, 'We're waiting to see how this shakes out. We don't want to get the technology wrong.' And I thought, wait — can you compute this in the language of accounting? It takes years to understand the depth of knowledge, the lingo, and accounting processes."

The team then examined whether the accounting memo use case was technically feasible with Sagnia's expertise and incorporating it into the technology stack would be critical for their partnership.

Though two foremost methods might have some promise, especially if used in combination, First, Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) provides the LLM with context and relevant information about accounting before prompting it to generate an answer. The danger is that if the system retrieved and applied it in the wrong situation, the LLM might still produce errors, potentially leading to more confusion or inaccurate output than the original. ### Business Plan

Sagnia and Shabahang built their business plan around what they viewed as the most attractive elements of audit and accounting work. These elements could not be easily outsourced or automated due to the complexity of the judgments involved. As they thought about it more, the audit knowledge base had to have both correctness and freshness to compile even the first draft. If their technology could help automate the first draft of an accounting memo, it could help move these accountants up the value chain. The team developed a go-to-market strategy that relied on strong domain expertise to help identify their target customers first. They first needed to offer a suite of compelling, effective products worth the required time investment to integrate into an accounting firm's workflow. The team considered using a different type of instrument: a convertible note, rather than a SAFE. The main difference between a SAFE and a convertible note is that a SAFE is an equity-like instrument that converts to equity in the next round, while a convertible note is a debt instrument that converts to equity in the future and carries an interest rate.
